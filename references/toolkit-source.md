## Slide 3: Strategic Thinking — Instructions

The Strategy Loop, step by step:

1. **Define** — Create the problem statement
2. **Split** — Deconstruct the problem into its drivers
3. **Analyse** — Analyse the data for each component driver
4. **Insight** — Create insight from the analyses and create your takeaways
5. **Story** — Put the pieces together and communicate in story form
6. **Decide** — Evaluate your options, select the best path and decide to execute
7. **Act / Iterate** — Prepare your implementation plan, mobilise teams and execute the strategy

## Slide 13: Driver Tree — Instructions

*SPLIT*

### Driver Tree diagram

From **PROBLEM STATEMENT**, break into components, drivers, and reasons:

- **Component 1**
  - Driver A → Reason A, Reason B, Reason C
  - Driver B
- **Component 2**
  - Driver C
- **Component 2** *(sic — as labelled on slide)*

### Steps

1. Deconstruct the problem using a driver tree method. Start by restating the problem.
2. Identify the component parts of the problem. Make each component distinct (limit overlap). *Example: improve traffic, increase conversion rate.*
3. Break down the component into the drivers of each leg of the problem. *Example: traffic drivers are paid ads, organic content, word of mouth.*
4. Continue to deconstruct each driver. Tip, there is no hard rule but 2 to 4 levels is manageable. *Example: paid ad focus is Facebook, YouTube, organic is blog and social media posts, and word of mouth is a referral campaign.*

## Slide 36: Unit Economics — Instructions

*ANALYSE*

'Unit Economics' examines the ongoing profit (revenues less cost) over life of the customer vs. the cost to acquire that customer.

### Metrics

1. **LTV (Lifetime Value)** — Sum of monthly profit per customer over the tenure of the customer.
2. **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)** — Upfront cost to acquire customer.
3. **LTV:CAC Ratio** — Ratio of customer life value to customer acquisition cost.
4. **CAC Payback Period** — Time it takes to fully cover customer acquisition costs.

## Slide 42: Cashflow — Instructions

*ANALYSE*

Cashflow framework is a method of valuation of a company or opportunity by calculating the sum of cashflows (discounted to today).

Timeline chart across periods `1, 2, 3, … n`:

- **Positive Cashflows** — Profits (above axis)
- **Negative Cashflows** — Investment & Losses (below axis)

### NPV
*Net present value*

Sum of discounted net cashflows less initial investment.

**Formula variables:**
- N = total number of periods
- n = a single period between 0 and N
- Cashflow = cashflow in period n (revenue less costs)
- r = discount rate, used to discount cashflow value to today

Cashflows are typically (low or negative) in early years and positive in later years. The goal is to ensure a positive NPV over a selected period of time (for example total profits less investment over 5 years).

## Slide 44: ROIC — Instructions

*ANALYSE*

Return on invested capital (ROIC) is a method to evaluate performance of a business and its growth levers. It measures how efficient a business is at investing its capital by reviewing the relationship between a dollar invested and its resulting profit.

**RETURN ON INVESTED CAPITAL (ROIC)** — tree structure:

| Branch | Sub-branch | Component | Drivers |
|--------|------------|-----------|---------|
| Operating Profit | Revenue | — | Units, Price |
| Operating Profit | Cost of Goods Sold | — | Units, Rate |
| Operating Profit | Operating Expenses | SG&A | Marketing, Rent, Labour |
| Pre-tax Net Invested Capital | Working Capital | Current Assets | Acc. Rec., Inventory |
| Pre-tax Net Invested Capital | Working Capital | Current Liabilities | Acc. Pay. |
| Pre-tax Net Invested Capital | Fixed Capital | PP&E | Fixed Assets |

**Callouts:**
- Operating profit tracks the drivers of net sales and ongoing expenses.
- Invested capital is the sum of short term liquid 'working capital' and larger asset investment 'fixed capital'.

## Slide 51: 5W + H — Instructions

*ANALYSE*

The 5w+H framework is a tool for investigating and troubleshooting a problem by looking at the problem from different angles with the goal of finding the root cause of the issue.

### 5W + H
*Investigating the answer, solving the mystery*

| Spoke | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **who** | Who is involved in the problem (causing, impacted, resolving)? |
| **what** | What is the problem? State the problem clearly (using methods in 'Define' section). |
| **when** | When is the duration (length of time) or frequency (one time vs. regularly) of the problem. |
| **where** | Where is the problem? Location and adjacent areas to where the problem is occurring. |
| **why** | Why did the problem occur? Use the 5Whys method to understand the underlying reason for the problem. |
| **how** | How do can we solve the problem? Determine the quick fix, and longer term solutions for solving the problem. |

## Slide 107: HV Logic Framework / instructions

*STORY*

### Minto Pyramid Part II – Horizontal & Vertical Logic

**GOVERNING THOUGHT**

- 1. Argument
  - 1.1 Evidence
  - 1.2 Evidence
- 2. Argument
  - 2.1 Evidence
  - 2.2 Evidence
- 3. Argument
  - 3.1 Evidence
  - 3.2 Evidence

### Horizontal Logic

Arguments can be presented as 'parts of a whole' or 'in sequence':

- **Parts of a whole** — each argument point supports the governing thought, or
- **In sequence** — each argument point builds on the last point to arrive at a conclusion.

### Vertical Logic

Each layer of the pyramid answers the question posed by the layer above it. The reader is typically asking the question "why is that" or "how is that done" which is answered in the layer below.

Ref. Barbara Minto, The Minto Pyramid Principle.

## Slide 108: MECE Framework / instructions

*STORY*

### Minto Pyramid Part III – Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive

**GOVERNING THOUGHT**

- 1. Argument
  - 1.1 Evidence
  - 1.2 Evidence
- 2. Argument
  - 2.1 Evidence
  - 2.2 Evidence
- 3. Argument
  - 3.1 Evidence
  - 3.2 Evidence

### Collectively Exhaustive (across Arguments)

- Argument points 'as a whole' resolves the problem statement.
- Your arguments in total are "complete" in supporting the big idea of your strategy.

### Mutually Exclusive (across Arguments / Evidence)

- Each argument point and supporting evidence is distinctive, and don't overlap.
- Your arguments are distinctly different from one another.

## Slide 111: Story Spine instructions

*STORY*

The Story Spine is a model for a structured story, typically used in screenplays and movies but can be adapted for any story.

### The Story Spine aka Pixar Storytelling Framework

| Line | Act |
|---|---|
| Once upon a time there was _ . | Beginning |
| Every day, _ . | |
| But, one day _ . | Event |
| Because of that, _ . | |
| Because of that, _ . | Middle |
| Until finally _ . | Climax |

Ref. Kenn Adams, The Story Spine

*Note: This model works better when you want to describe events that have occurred – much like a case study. In a forward looking strategy – it's better to have the "climax" (the end state) described early on in the deck.*

1. **Beginning**: Introducing the context of your story – the facts around the business, its market and its current status.
2. **Event**: However, something happens that causes problems for the business or product or prevents it from reaching its intended goals.
3. **Middle**: And because of that, you pursue a series of actions (strategic imperatives) in order to resolve this problem and achieve your goal.
4. **Climax**: Then finally, we achieve the goal we originally set out to deliver. At this point, we can describe what comes next.
